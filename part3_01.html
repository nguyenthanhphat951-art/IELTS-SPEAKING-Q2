Question 1 of 4 Comparison / Trend
"In your country, do people nowadays cook at home as frequently as people did in the past?"

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

From what I can observe, home cooking has declined significantly compared to previous generations, particularly in urban areas. The main drivers are busier work schedules, longer commuting times, and the extraordinary convenience of food delivery apps, which have made it possible to have restaurant-quality food delivered to your door within thirty minutes. That said, I think the picture is more nuanced than a simple decline. The pandemic actually sparked a renewed interest in home cooking among younger people, many of whom discovered the satisfaction of preparing meals from scratch for the first time. And in rural areas and among older generations, home cooking remains very much the norm. So I would say the trend is towards less frequent home cooking overall, but with pockets of genuine revival.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

Personally, I find it quite rude and disheartening. Mealtimes are one of the few occasions in modern life when people naturally gather together, and using a phone during that time sends a clear message that whatever is on the screen is more important than the people you are with. Research has shown that even the presence of a phone on the table - even if it is face down and silent - reduces the quality of conversation and connection between people. That said, I think context matters. If someone is expecting an urgent call or needs to check something important, that is understandable. But habitual, mindless scrolling during a meal is something I think we should all make a conscious effort to avoid.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

I think there are several reasons. First, special occasions are inherently associated with celebration and indulgence - people feel that the day deserves something extraordinary, and a lavish meal is one of the most universal ways to mark that. Second, an expensive meal signals the importance of the occasion and the people being honoured. If you are celebrating a parent's milestone birthday or a wedding anniversary, choosing a fine restaurant communicates how much you value that person and that moment. Third, and perhaps most importantly, food is deeply tied to memory and emotion. A truly exceptional meal creates a sensory experience that people remember for years, which makes the investment feel worthwhile. The meal becomes part of the story of the occasion.

There are some fascinating differences. Vietnamese special occasion food tends to be deeply symbolic and labour-intensive - dishes like banh chung for Tet or xoi gac for weddings carry specific cultural meanings and are often prepared by the whole family together, which makes the process itself part of the celebration. In contrast, special occasion food in many Western countries, while certainly festive, tends to be more standardised and commercially available - a Christmas turkey or a birthday cake can be purchased ready-made from a supermarket. Another difference is the communal nature of Vietnamese festive dining - dishes are placed in the centre of the table and shared by everyone, which reflects a collectivist cultural value, whereas Western dining often involves individual servings. Both approaches are beautiful in their own way, but they reflect quite different cultural values around food and celebration.
