✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

I think it is extremely important, and it is an area where many educational systems still fall short. Traditional schooling tends to prioritise a narrow range of academic skills - primarily literacy and numeracy - and students who excel in these areas are recognised and rewarded, while those whose strengths lie elsewhere - in music, sport, visual arts, practical skills, or social intelligence - may feel overlooked or undervalued. This is a significant waste of human potential. Schools that actively identify and nurture the diverse talents of their students not only help those students develop confidence and a sense of purpose, but also prepare them for a diverse economy that genuinely needs people with a wide range of skills and abilities. The important caveat is that talent identification should be done carefully and without premature labelling - children develop at different rates, and what looks like a lack of talent at age ten may simply be a talent that has not yet been discovered or developed.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

Modern society is extraordinarily complex and multifaceted, and it genuinely needs people with a vast range of different talents to function well. The most obvious need is for technical and scientific talent - engineers, doctors, programmers, and researchers who drive technological progress and solve complex problems. But equally essential are creative talents: artists, designers, writers, and musicians who enrich our cultural life and help us make sense of the world. Social talents - the ability to care for others, build communities, teach, and lead - are fundamental to social cohesion and well-being. And practical talents - skilled tradespeople, farmers, builders, and craftspeople - underpin the physical infrastructure of society. Innovation, which is the engine of economic and social progress, often emerges precisely from the intersection of different types of talent - when a scientist collaborates with a designer, or when an engineer thinks like an artist. A society that values and develops only a narrow range of talents impoverishes itself in ways that are difficult to quantify but deeply felt.
