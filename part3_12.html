Question 1 of 2 General Knowledge / Descriptive
"What kinds of facilities do big cities have?"
🎯 Answer Direction & Strategy
  • Describe a range of facilities available in big cities.
  • Mention both the advantages these facilities offer and any associated issues.
  • Use specific examples.
💡 Ideas to Use in Your Answer
  • Big cities offer world-class healthcare facilities, including specialist hospitals and medical centres.
  • They have extensive educational institutions, from universities to international schools.
  • Cultural facilities like museums, theatres, concert halls, and galleries are concentrated in cities.
  • Transport infrastructure - airports, metro systems, bus networks - makes cities highly connected.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

Big cities offer an extraordinary range of facilities that smaller towns and rural areas simply cannot match. In terms of healthcare, major cities typically have world-class hospitals with specialist departments, cutting-edge medical technology, and access to the best medical professionals. Educationally, they concentrate the best universities, international schools, and research institutions. Culturally, cities are home to museums, art galleries, theatres, concert halls, and a vibrant restaurant and entertainment scene that reflects the diversity of their populations. Their transport infrastructure - airports, metro systems, bus networks, and increasingly, cycling infrastructure - makes them highly connected both internally and to the wider world. And economically, cities offer the greatest concentration of employment opportunities across virtually every sector. The challenge, of course, is that these facilities come with significant costs: high property prices, traffic congestion, pollution, and social inequality.

Question 2 of 2 Opinion / Analysis
"What are the disadvantages of living in a very famous city?"
🎯 Answer Direction & Strategy
  • ● Consider economic, social, and environmental dimensions.
  • Use specific examples where possible.
💡 Ideas to Use in Your Answer
  • Famous cities tend to have extremely high costs of living, particularly for housing.
  • They are often overcrowded, with traffic congestion, noise pollution, and limited green space.
  • The influx of tourists can disrupt local communities and drive up prices for residents.
  • Famous cities can feel impersonal and anonymous, making it difficult to build genuine community

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

Living in a very famous city comes with some significant drawbacks that are easy to overlook when you are visiting as a tourist. The most immediate is cost: famous cities - think Paris, London, New York, or Tokyo - tend to have extremely high costs of living, particularly for housing. Renting or buying property in these cities can consume a disproportionate share of income, leaving less for other aspects of life. Overcrowding is another major issue: famous cities attract enormous numbers of tourists, which can make everyday activities like using public transport, visiting local shops, or simply walking through the streets a frustrating experience. The tourist economy can also drive up prices and displace local businesses and residents. And perhaps most subtly, very large, famous cities can feel impersonal and anonymous - it can be surprisingly difficult to build genuine community connections when you are surrounded by millions of people who are constantly moving in and out.
