✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

People start businesses for a fascinating variety of reasons. For some, the primary motivation is financial - the desire for greater earning potential and financial independence than traditional employment can offer. For others, it is about pursuing a passion or solving a problem they have personally experienced and believe they can address better than existing solutions. There is also a significant push factor: many entrepreneurs start businesses because they are dissatisfied with traditional employment - frustrated by lack of autonomy, limited career progression, or a work culture that does not align with their values. And for some, particularly those who have achieved financial security, starting a business is about creating something meaningful, building a legacy, and making a positive contribution to their community or industry. The most successful entrepreneurs tend to be driven by a combination of these motivations rather than any single one.

Question 2 of 3 Opinion / Analysis
"What makes a business successful?"
🎯 Answer Direction & Strategy
  • ● Give specific examples where possible.
  • Prioritise the most important factors.
💡 Ideas to Use in Your Answer
  • A clear understanding of the target market and a product or service that genuinely meets their needs.
  • Strong leadership and a talented, motivated team.
  • Financial discipline - managing cash flow, controlling costs, and reinvesting profits wisely.
  • The ability to adapt to changing market conditions and customer needs.
🔗 Key Vocabulary
build upstand out fromcarry out (strategy)deal with (challenges)look intocome up withbusiness successmarket understandingleadership qualityfinancial managementcompetitive advantage
✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

Business success is multifactorial, but I think a few elements are particularly critical. First and foremost, a successful business must offer something that genuinely meets a real need or desire in the market - no amount of marketing or operational excellence can compensate for a product or service that people do not actually want. Second, strong leadership is essential: the ability to inspire and retain talented people, make sound strategic decisions, and navigate challenges with resilience and creativity. Third, financial discipline - understanding cash flow, managing costs, and making wise investment decisions - is often what separates businesses that survive from those that fail, even when the underlying idea is good. And finally, adaptability: the ability to respond to changing market conditions, customer feedback, and competitive pressures is increasingly important in a rapidly evolving business environment.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

I think governments should provide some form of support to start-ups, but with important caveats. The case for support is strong: start-ups are engines of innovation and job creation, and many promising businesses fail not because of bad ideas but because of lack of access to capital in the critical early stages. Government support can help bridge this gap and create a more dynamic, innovative economy. However, I think direct financial grants should be targeted carefully and subject to rigorous evaluation to avoid wasting public funds on unviable businesses. In many cases, non-financial forms of support may be more effective: simplifying regulations for new businesses, providing mentorship and business development resources, offering tax incentives for early-stage investment, and creating ecosystems where start-ups can connect with experienced entrepreneurs and investors. The goal should be to create conditions in which good ideas can flourish, rather than simply writing cheques.
