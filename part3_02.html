"Why are people keen on buying new electronic devices?"

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

I think the reasons are a mix of genuine and manufactured. On the genuine side, new electronic devices often offer real improvements - better cameras, faster processors, longer battery life - that make a tangible difference to daily life. On the manufactured side, the technology industry is extraordinarily skilled at creating desire through marketing, product launches, and the social currency of owning the latest device. There is also the phenomenon of planned obsolescence, where older devices are deliberately slowed down or denied software updates, effectively forcing users to upgrade. And of course, social pressure plays a role - people do not want to be seen using an outdated phone when everyone around them has the latest model. It is a complex mix of genuine need and carefully engineered desire.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

Electronic devices have had a profound and genuinely double-edged impact on people's lives. On the positive side, they have revolutionised communication, making it possible to connect with anyone anywhere in the world instantly. They have democratised access to information and education - a student in a remote village can now access the same knowledge as someone in a major city. They have also transformed productivity, enabling people to work more flexibly and efficiently. On the negative side, however, the evidence is growing that excessive device use is associated with reduced attention spans, disrupted sleep patterns, increased anxiety, and a paradoxical sense of social isolation despite being constantly connected. The challenge for individuals and society is to harness the extraordinary benefits of these devices while developing the self-awareness and discipline to manage their downsides.

AI offers some extraordinary advantages. In medicine, AI systems can analyse medical images and patient data with a speed and accuracy that surpasses human doctors in certain tasks, potentially saving lives through earlier and more accurate diagnoses. In education, AI can personalise learning experiences to individual students' needs. And in everyday life, AI-powered tools are making us more productive and efficient. However, the concerns are equally significant. The displacement of workers through automation is a serious and growing issue, particularly for people in routine or manual jobs who may lack the skills to transition to new roles. There are also profound concerns about data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the concentration of AI power in the hands of a small number of large corporations. The challenge is to develop AI in a way that maximises its benefits while actively addressing these risks through thoughtful regulation and investment in human skills development.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

I think there is a genuine and growing concern about over-reliance, yes. From what I observe among students, many are using AI tools like ChatGPT to generate essays, solve problems, and complete assignments that they should be working through themselves. The danger is that this bypasses the very cognitive processes that education is designed to develop - critical thinking, analytical reasoning, and the ability to construct and communicate ideas clearly. If students consistently outsource these processes to AI, they may graduate with qualifications but without the underlying skills those qualifications are supposed to represent. That said, I do not think AI is inherently harmful in education. Used thoughtfully - for research, generating ideas to critique, or getting feedback on drafts - it can be a powerful learning tool. The issue is the lack of clear guidance and boundaries around its use, which is something educators and institutions urgently need to address.
