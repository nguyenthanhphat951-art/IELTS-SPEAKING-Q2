✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

Living preferences in Vietnam vary considerably by generation, income, and location. Among younger urban professionals, modern apartments in city centres or well-connected suburban areas are increasingly popular, valued for their convenience, security, and modern amenities. Older generations, particularly those with rural roots, often prefer traditional houses with gardens and more space, reflecting a deeper connection to land and nature. There is also a strong cultural preference for living close to family - multigenerational households remain common, and many Vietnamese people choose their home based partly on proximity to parents or in-laws. As cities expand and central property prices rise, suburban living is becoming increasingly common, with many families trading city-centre convenience for more space and a quieter environment.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

The differences are quite significant. City homes in Vietnam tend to be smaller, more expensive, and predominantly in apartment buildings or narrow townhouses - a reflection of high land prices and dense urban populations. They offer excellent access to amenities, services, schools, and employment, but often at the cost of space, privacy, and connection to nature. Countryside homes, by contrast, are typically much larger, more affordable, and more traditional in design - often single-storey houses with gardens, courtyards, and sometimes agricultural land. They offer a quieter, more spacious lifestyle with a stronger sense of community and connection to nature, but with more limited access to services and economic opportunities. These differences reflect broader social and economic inequalities between urban and rural Vietnam, and they are a significant driver of the ongoing migration of young people from rural areas to cities.
