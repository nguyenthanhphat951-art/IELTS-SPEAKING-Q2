Question 1 of 2 Comparison
"How do young and old people use mobile phones differently?"
🎯 Answer Direction & Strategy
  • ● Explain the reasons behind these differences.
  • Avoid stereotyping - acknowledge that individuals vary.
💡 Ideas to Use in Your Answer
  • Young people tend to use phones primarily for social media, entertainment, and communication with
  • Older people are more likely to use phones primarily for calls, messaging family, and practical tasks.
  • Young people are generally more comfortable with a wider range of apps and features.
  • Older people may use phones less frequently and for shorter periods, though this is changing rapidly.

make use ofrely onkeep up withdeal withcarry onstand out fromdigital literacyapp usagecommunication habitgenerational differencesmartphone behaviour
✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

The differences are quite pronounced, though they are narrowing as older generations become more digitally literate. Young people tend to use their phones as all-encompassing lifestyle devices - for social media, entertainment, gaming, shopping, navigation, and communication with peers through a wide range of apps. They are typically comfortable with a broad range of features and adopt new apps and platforms quickly. Older people, particularly those over sixty, tend to use phones more selectively - primarily for phone calls, text messages, and perhaps a few practical apps like maps or banking. They may be less comfortable with newer features and more cautious about privacy and security. However, I think it is important not to overgeneralise: there are many older people who are highly digitally literate and many young people who use their phones quite minimally. The differences reflect familiarity and habit as much as age itself.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

I think some laws on mobile phone use are clearly necessary and justified. The most obvious example is the prohibition on using handheld phones while driving, which has been shown to significantly reduce accidents and save lives. This is a case where the safety risk is so clear and the harm to others so direct that legal restriction is entirely appropriate. Restrictions in certain settings - schools, hospitals, courtrooms, places of worship - are also reasonable and widely accepted. However, I am more cautious about overly broad restrictions on phone use in general public spaces. These would be difficult to enforce, potentially infringe on personal freedom, and may not be the most effective approach to the underlying issues. For many phone-related social problems - rudeness, distraction, addiction - I think education, cultural norms, and individual self-discipline are more appropriate tools than legislation.
