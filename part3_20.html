Question 1 of 2 Opinion / General Knowledge
"When would parents feel proud of their children?"
🎯 Answer Direction & Strategy
  • Describe a range of situations that make parents proud.
  • Consider both achievement-based and character-based sources of pride.
  • Mention that pride can come from unexpected places.
💡 Ideas to Use in Your Answer
  • Academic achievements like good grades, graduation, or gaining admission to a prestigious institution.
  • Demonstrating good character: kindness, honesty, resilience, and compassion towards others.
  • Overcoming personal challenges or adversity with courage and determination.
  • Pursuing their passions and developing their unique talents and identity.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

Parents feel proud of their children in a wonderfully wide range of situations. The most obvious are academic and professional achievements - good exam results, graduation, gaining admission to a prestigious university, or landing an important job. These are milestones that parents can point to with clear, tangible pride. But I think the deepest parental pride often comes from witnessing their child's character rather than their achievements. Seeing a child show genuine kindness to someone who is struggling, stand up for what is right even when it is difficult, or demonstrate resilience in the face of adversity - these moments often move parents more profoundly than any academic result. Parents also feel proud when their children pursue their passions with genuine commitment, even if those passions are unconventional. Ultimately, I think most parents are proudest when they see their child becoming a good, capable, and happy person - whatever form that takes.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

I think rewarding children too frequently can actually be counterproductive, and there is interesting psychological research to support this. When children are rewarded for everything - including activities they would naturally enjoy - it can undermine their intrinsic motivation. They begin to do things for the reward rather than for the inherent satisfaction of the activity, and when the reward is removed, their engagement drops. This is sometimes called the "overjustification effect." That said, I do not think rewards are inherently harmful - they can be very effective when used thoughtfully. The key is to make rewards specific, meaningful, and tied to genuine effort or achievement rather than routine behaviour. And the ultimate goal should be to help children develop internal motivation - a sense of pride and satisfaction in their own accomplishments - rather than dependence on external validation. Praise that focuses on effort and process rather than outcome tends to be more beneficial than material rewards.
