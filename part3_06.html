✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

The situation in Vietnam is improving rapidly, though there is still significant room for growth. English is by far the most widely studied foreign language, and it is now taught from primary school level, which means younger generations have much greater exposure than their parents did. In major cities like Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, you will find many young professionals who can communicate effectively in English. However, conversational fluency at a high level remains relatively uncommon outside of educated urban populations. Beyond English, there is growing interest in Chinese, Japanese, and Korean, driven by strong economic ties with China and Japan and the enormous cultural influence of Korean pop culture. I think Vietnam is on a positive trajectory in terms of foreign language ability, but there is still work to be done in improving the quality of language education, particularly in rural areas.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

There are several compelling reasons. From a neurological perspective, children's brains have significantly greater plasticity - they form new neural connections more rapidly and efficiently than adult brains, which is why children can acquire languages and new skills with remarkable speed and naturalness. Psychologically, children are also far less inhibited by fear of failure or embarrassment. They are willing to make mistakes, try again, and experiment without the self-consciousness that often holds adults back. They also have fewer competing demands on their time and attention, which allows for more immersive, sustained learning. That said, adults are not without advantages. We have stronger metacognitive skills - we know how we learn best - and we can draw on a vast store of prior knowledge to contextualise new information. Adult motivation, when it is strong, can also be a powerful driver of learning. The key for adults is to approach learning with the same openness and willingness to make mistakes that children naturally possess.

✅ Band 7+ Model Answer

I respectfully disagree with that view. While translation technology has improved dramatically and can certainly handle basic communication needs, I think it fundamentally misunderstands what language learning is about. Speaking someone's language is not just about transmitting information - it is about accessing their culture, their humour, their way of seeing the world. When you speak to someone in their native language, even imperfectly, you create a human connection that no translation app can replicate. There is also the cognitive dimension: research consistently shows that learning a language develops memory, attention, and problem-solving skills that have value far beyond communication. And in professional contexts, the ability to communicate directly in another language remains a significant competitive advantage. Technology is a wonderful tool for language learners, but it is a supplement to language learning, not a substitute for it.
